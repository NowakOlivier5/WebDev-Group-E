A little bit about marine life history

Since Homo sapiens began exploiting marine environments, human activities have driven significant marine animal extinctions. Early overfishing, hunting, and habitat destruction disrupted ecosystems. Species like the Steller's sea cow and Caribbean monk seal were hunted to extinction. Industrialization intensified the impact, with pollution, overfishing, and climate change accelerating declines. Coral reefs, crucial habitats, have been devastated by warming seas and acidification. Although conservation efforts have emerged, over 30% of marine species face extinction, highlighting humanity’s profound influence on marine biodiversity over millennia.

On the agenda...

Join our cause!

The UN fights marine animal extinction by bringing countries together to protect the oceans. Through agreements like UNCLOS, it tackles overfishing, pollution, and habitat loss. It promotes marine protected areas, addresses climate change through the Paris Agreement, and pushes for cleaner seas with campaigns against plastic pollution. By setting rules, funding projects, and raising awareness, the UN ensures marine life gets a chance to thrive.

Some big allies!

Some big NGOs protect marine animals from extinction, like WWF (World Wildlife Fund), which fights overfishing and promotes sustainable oceans. Oceana focuses on policies to save marine species and habitats. Sea Shepherd actively stops illegal fishing and whaling. Greenpeace campaigns against pollution and destructive fishing practices. Marine Conservation Institute creates marine protected areas. These groups push governments, raise awareness, and take direct action to keep marine animals safe.

Best case scenario!

Clean, healthy oceans are a win for everyone, especially marine animals. Clear waters mean less pollution, so creatures like turtles don’t mistake plastic for food. Healthy oceans have thriving coral reefs and seagrass beds, giving fish, crabs, and dolphins safe places to live and eat. Plus, balanced ecosystems mean fewer extinctions and better biodiversity. For humans, it’s fresh seafood, better climate regulation, and beautiful oceans full of life to enjoy!
